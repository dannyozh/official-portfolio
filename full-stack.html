Building from back to front: WWJD

  • by:Daniel

Like many tech enthusiasts, I consider Steve Jobs to be a hero. I loved him for a variety of reasons, but perhaps most pertinently, for his work on a little app called Garageband. I've lost count the number of times I have viewed the video of Jobs demonstrating the app's 'digital guitar amplifiers' alongside another hero of mine, John Mayer. The idea that you could hook up a guitar to your Macbook - then called the Powerbook - record a track and share it with fellow music lovers was incredibly novel.

Now, I had the opportunity to build my own full-stack app, or as I saw it, a chance to build something that would appear novel to the 14-year-old me. With the mindset of "what would Jobs do?" firmly on my mind, I went on to create a journal app called Moodlogger, which is designed to be a companion for those sufferening from mental health illnesses. The app's back-end component comprises daily journals where users can key in their mood 'score' and jot down their feelings. According to an aggregate of their mood scores as well as their logs, the app would auto-generate inspirational quotes that I hope users would find timely in their moments of need.

Now, I had the opportunity to build my own full-stack app, or as I saw it, a chance to build something that would appear novel to the 14-year-old me.

On the front-end side, I was focused on making the app as personalised as possible. Elements that contributed to this effect include a changing landscape dependent on the time of day, a mood graph in soothing pastel colours, plus a compilation of the user's logs that have a similar colour to Apple's popular Notes application.

Just as how Garageband opened my eyes to the possibilities of recording guitar ideas straight to one's Macbook, Moodlogger was created to similarly let those relieve the burderning thoughts of a heavy mind.
